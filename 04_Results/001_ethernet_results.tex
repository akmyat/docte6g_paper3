\subsubsection{Deployment over Ethernet Connectivity}

Figure \ref{fig:ethernet_telemetry_results} presents latency measurements for telemetry messages under three Ethernet data rates: 100 Mbps, 1 Gbps, and 10 Gbps. Telemetry delays increase monotonically with QoS level, rising from 47 µs (QoS 0) to 207 µs (QoS 2) at 100 Mbps due to cumulative acknowledgment overhead. Higher link speeds yield lower delays, though improvements saturate at QoS 1 and QoS 2 because handshake latency dominates.

Operational message results (Fig. \ref{fig:ethernet_operations_results}) exhibit similar trends. While most operational messages remain below 30–90 µs under QoS 0 for all Ethernet speeds, QoS 1 and QoS 2 introduce substantial delay inflation. Notably, Rack Remove Package messages experience sporadic packet loss under QoS 0 due to its at-most-once delivery semantics; all loss disappears under QoS 1/2, confirming correct reliability handling. QoS 2 delays exceed 500 µs at 100 Mbps but drop below 200 µs at 10 Gbps.

Control message performance is shown in Fig. \ref{fig:ethernet_operations_results}. Although payloads are small (112 bytes for assignment, 37 bytes for completion), QoS 1 and QoS 2 incur substantial latency due to retransmission and handshake timing. Only QoS 0 maintains sub-20 ms delay across all Ethernet configurations. Under QoS 1/2, robot mobility and link variability further exacerbate delays, causing assignment commands to frequently exceed 70 ms. These findings indicate that Ethernet with QoS 0 meets latency requirements ($<20$ ms) for robot control, but QoS 1–2 introduce excessive delay unless reliability is externally guaranteed.
