\subsubsection{Deployment over WiFi Connectivity}

Wi-Fi performance reflects contention-based medium access and PHY-layer variability. Figure \ref{fig:wifi_telemetry_results} shows telemetry results for 802.11n, 802.11ac, and 802.11ax. Compared to Ethernet, all Wi-Fi configurations yield significantly higher latency. QoS 0 telemetry delays decrease from $\approx$ 1.30 ms (802.11n) to $\approx$ 1.11 ms (802.11ax), while QoS 1 and QoS 2 consistently induce additional 0.3–0.6 ms overhead. PHY improvements reduce delay modestly, but acknowledgment exchange dominates for higher QoS levels.

Operational message results (Fig.  \ref{fig:wifi_operations_results}) demonstrate that package detection messages experience the highest delay due to larger payloads. Under QoS 0, detection delays fall from 2.5 ms (802.11n) to  $\approx$ 2.05 ms (802.11ac/ax); under QoS 2, they increase to 3.1–3.5 ms. Other operations remain below 1.3 ms across standards and QoS levels. These trends confirm that Wi-Fi PHY improvements reduce collision-related latency but cannot compensate for protocol-level handshake cost.

Figure  \ref{fig:wifi_control_results} shows control message performance. QoS 0 produces  $\approx$ 1.0 ms delay under 802.11n and  $\approx$ 0.83 ms under 802.11ac/ax. QoS 1/2 introduce modest increases to 1.1–1.3 ms, but performance remains well below Ethernet QoS-1/QoS-2 values. This reflects Wi-Fi’s lower baseline RTT despite higher jitter. Importantly, Wi-Fi meets the latency requirements for robot control ($<20 ms$) at all QoS levels, though reliability and jitter may still be concerns for safety-critical actuation
